# AI Policy Coverage Matrix: Gap Analysis
**11 policies · 29 risk subcategories · April 2026**

---

## What This Is

A systematic coverage analysis mapping 11 major AI governance frameworks against a 29-subcategory risk taxonomy. Policies scored 0–3 on each risk (0 = not addressed, 1 = mentioned only, 2 = specific provisions, 3 = provisions + enforcement). Analysis covers: EU AI Act, CA SB-53, Bletchley Declaration, G7 Hiroshima Principles and CoC, Anthropic RSP and FCF, OpenAI Preparedness Framework, DeepMind FSF, Google AI Principles, Meta RAI.

---

## The Headline Finding

**The governance frontier is in San Francisco, not Brussels.**

Private sector frameworks — built without legislative compulsion — are systematically more operationally advanced on the most dangerous AI risks than any binding law. This is not because governments failed. It is because frontier labs invented the risk taxonomy first, then created frameworks to address it, while legislators were still learning what to regulate.

The result is a profound structural fragility: the world's most consequential AI safety commitments rest on voluntary corporate policies that can be revised or abandoned without legal consequence. SB-53 and the Anthropic FCF represent the earliest working model for converting those voluntary commitments into legally enforceable obligations — but only for California-jurisdiction activities.

## Top 5 Most Under-Governed Risk Areas

### 1. Military Escalation / Autonomous Weapons — *Binding score: 0/3*
The EU AI Act explicitly excludes military and defence applications. No international treaty governs autonomous weapons. The only governance is two corporate principles documents (DeepMind FSF, Google AI Principles). The most consequential explicit governance exclusion in the dataset.

### 2. Strategic Instability — *Binding score: 0/3*
No binding instrument attempts to address AI racing dynamics. Only Anthropic has a voluntary commitment to not cutting safety corners in competition — and it cannot bind competitors. The meta-risk underlying all other frontier AI risks has no multilateral governance response.

### 3. Epistemic Collapse — *Max score: 1/3 across all policies*
Eight frameworks acknowledge that AI could erode shared epistemic foundations. Zero provide binding provisions beyond basic labelling requirements. Named everywhere. Governed nowhere.

### 4. Dependency Monoculture — *Average score: 0.09/3*
Global AI infrastructure is concentrating in 3–5 companies and 1–2 chipmakers. A failure, breach, or geopolitical disruption of this infrastructure has no governance framework. Supply chain security exists for chips (export controls) but not for AI service concentration.

### 5. Value Lock-In — *Binding score: 0/3*
The possibility that current AI development choices will permanently constrain future value pluralism is raised only by Anthropic's RSP. No binding law acknowledges that encoding specific values into AI systems at civilizational scale could foreclose future human choice.

---

## Top 5 Best-Governed Risk Areas

### 1. CBRN Misuse Enablement — *Average score: 1.82/3*
The one risk where binding law and private sector frameworks converge at high scores. SB-53 codified it as a statutory catastrophic risk; Anthropic, OpenAI, and DeepMind all built specific bio-uplift evaluation frameworks achieving score 3. The most operationally developed governance in the dataset.

### 2. Cyber Offense Acceleration — *Average score: 1.82/3*
Parallel to CBRN across frontier lab frameworks. Four private sector frameworks score 3. Binding law still lags (SB-53 scores 1 on this; EU AI Act scores 1).

### 3. Loss of Human Oversight — *EU AI Act: 3/3*
EU AI Act Article 14 is the most technically specific human oversight mandate in any binding instrument — requiring specific technical capabilities to override, halt, and correct high-risk AI, backed by penalties. Nine of 11 policies score ≥1.

### 4. Emergent Capabilities — *Private sector max: 3/3*
Four private sector frameworks achieve score 3 through tier-based capability evaluation systems (RSP, Preparedness, FCF, FSF). The gap: binding law scores only 1 via compute thresholds. Well-governed among voluntary frameworks; poorly governed in law.

### 5. Persuasion / Behavioral Manipulation — *EU AI Act: 3/3*
EU AI Act Art. 5's prohibition on subliminal manipulation, backed by €35M/7% global turnover penalties, is the most robustly enforced provision in the dataset. Uniquely, binding law outperforms private sector frameworks on this category.

## Nine Risks with No Substantive Governance (Max Score ≤ 1 Across All Policies)

1. **Dependency monoculture** — AI infrastructure concentration
2. **Synthetic data loops** — Model collapse from AI-on-AI training
3. **Regulatory capture** — Labs shaping governance to entrench dominance
4. **Epistemic collapse** — Erosion of shared reality
5. **Labor polarization** — Structural job displacement
6. **Productivity paradox** — Who captures the AI dividend
7. **Deskilling** — Human cognitive atrophy
8. **Social isolation** — AI companionship substituting human connection
9. **Cultural homogenization** — Global AI flattening linguistic/cultural diversity

The entire economic-systemic category (labor, productivity, concentration) and human-societal category (deskilling, isolation, homogenization) are policy deserts. Governance frameworks have collectively decided these are not their problem.

---

## Three Surprising Findings

**1. SB-53 is more technically precise on existential AI risk than the EU AI Act.** A single California statute is the first binding law anywhere to codify "deceptive model behavior" and "loss of control" as named legal categories. The 28-nation EU AI Act — covering 450 million people — leaves both as recital acknowledgements only.

**2. The entire multilateral apparatus (Bletchley + G7) adds up to one policy stated twice.** The Bletchley Declaration, G7 Hiroshima Principles, and G7 Hiroshima CoC cover almost identical conceptual ground at almost identical levels of abstraction, all scoring ≤1 on every subcategory. Their value is in naming risks and creating political momentum, not in governing them.

**3. Meta is the only policy source to substantively address economic concentration.** By framing its open-source release programme as an anti-concentration commitment, Meta achieves score 2 on economic-concentration — the only policy to do so. Whether this is genuine governance or competitive positioning dressed as principle is a legitimate question.

---

## What Comes Next

This analysis covers 11 of 25 frameworks in scope. The 14 remaining — including China's GenAI Measures, NIST AI RMF, UNESCO AI Ethics, CoE AI Convention, and ISO standards — will shift some of these findings. China's binding measures in particular will test whether the "binding law is behind" pattern holds when non-Western regulatory approaches are included.

The more important question is structural: how do we convert the operational sophistication of private sector frontier safety frameworks into durable legal obligations that don't depend on corporate continuity or goodwill? The FCF/SB-53 architecture points toward one answer.
