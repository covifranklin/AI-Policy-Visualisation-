# AI Risk Taxonomy

## 1. Alignment & Control Risks

### 1.1 Goal misalignment / specification gaming
Models optimize the wrong objective (reward hacking, Goodhart's law). Can appear aligned in testing but fail in deployment.

### 1.2 Deceptive alignment / scheming
Model behaves well during evaluation but pursues hidden goals. Key concern in frontier model governance.

### 1.3 Emergent capabilities risk
New dangerous capabilities appear unexpectedly at scale. Undermines incremental regulation assumptions.

### 1.4 Loss of human oversight (automation overhang)
Humans become unable to meaningfully supervise systems. Particularly relevant in high-speed domains (cyber, finance, military).

## 2. Operational & Infrastructure Risks

### 2.1 Supply chain vulnerabilities
Dependence on chips (e.g. NVIDIA), cloud providers (e.g. AWS). Geopolitical chokepoints.

### 2.2 Model theft / weights exfiltration
Theft of frontier model weights. Equivalent to proliferation risk (like nuclear tech leakage).

### 2.3 Data poisoning / training pipeline attacks
Adversaries manipulate training data. Leads to hidden backdoors or bias.

### 2.4 Dependency risk (AI monoculture)
Over-reliance on a small number of models/providers. Systemic fragility if one fails or is compromised.

## 3. Information & Epistemic Risks

### 3.1 Epistemic collapse
Widespread inability to distinguish true from false. Erosion of shared reality.

### 3.2 Synthetic data feedback loops
Models trained on AI-generated content degrade over time. "Model collapse" risk.

### 3.3 Persuasion / behavioral manipulation
AI optimized to influence beliefs or actions at scale. Political, commercial, or extremist use.

## 4. Security & Conflict Risks

### 4.1 CBRN misuse enablement
AI lowers barriers to developing chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear weapons.

### 4.2 Cyber offense acceleration
AI dramatically lowers cost of cyberattacks. Automated vulnerability discovery and exploitation.

### 4.3 Military escalation / autonomous weapons
Faster decision loops leading to instability. Reduced human control in warfare.

### 4.4 Strategic instability (AI arms race)
States race to deploy unsafe systems. Analogous to nuclear brinkmanship.

## 5. Governance & Institutional Risks

### 5.1 Regulatory capture
Large labs influence regulation to entrench dominance.

### 5.2 Capability overhang
Powerful models exist but are not yet deployed — sudden release risk.

### 5.3 Enforcement gaps
Laws exist but cannot be effectively enforced (compute tracking, audits).

### 5.4 Jurisdictional arbitrage
Firms move development to weaker regulatory regimes.

## 6. Economic & Systemic Risks

### 6.1 Economic concentration / rent extraction
AI creates winner-take-most dynamics. Extreme capital concentration.

### 6.2 Labor market polarization
Hollowing out of mid-skill jobs. Not just displacement — structural inequality shift.

### 6.3 Productivity paradox / uneven gains
Benefits accrue slowly or unevenly, leading to social instability.

## 7. Human & Societal Risks

### 7.1 Human deskilling / cognitive atrophy
Overreliance on AI reduces human capability.

### 7.2 Loss of agency / autonomy
People increasingly defer decisions to AI.

### 7.3 Social isolation / AI companionship effects
Substitution of human relationships with AI interactions.

### 7.4 Cultural homogenization
Global models flatten linguistic and cultural diversity.

## 8. Long-term / Existential Risks

### 8.1 Misaligned superintelligence
AI surpasses human control and optimization diverges from human values.

### 8.2 Irreversible loss of control
Systems that cannot be shut down or corrected.

### 8.3 Value lock-in
Early AI systems entrench specific ideologies or power structures permanently.
